As an example, consider the R dataset HairEyeColor. This records the hair and eye colour from a survey of 592 students at the University of Delaware (N.B. it also records “Sex” whose values are fake).

knitr::kable(apply(HairEyeColor, 1:2, sum))
Brown Blue Hazel Green
Black 68 20 15 5
Brown 119 84 54 29
Red 26 17 14 14
Blond 7 94 10 16

An eikosgram implicitly distinguishes one of the variates as a “response”, the other(s) being the variate(s) upon which we condition. More precisely, eikosograms implicitly assume that interest lies in \[Pr(Y = y | X = x)\] where Y is the “response” and “X” is the “conditioning” variate.

If we take “Hair” to be the response, we would call the eikos function with conditioning variate “Eye” as follows:

library(eikos)
eikos("Hair", "Eye", data = HairEyeColor, xaxs=FALSE, yaxs=FALSE)

## Called from: eikos_data(y, x, data, marginalize)

Interchanging the two variates gives “Eye” as the response and “Hair” as the conditioning variate:

eikos("Eye", "Hair", data=HairEyeColor, xaxs=FALSE, yaxs=FALSE)

## Called from: eikos_data(y, x, data, marginalize)

We could also write the variates as a formula:

eikos(Eye~Hair, data=HairEyeColor)

## Called from: eikos_data(y, x, data, marginalize)

We could add the values of the proportions on the axes by setting xaxs=TRUE, yaxs=TRUE:

eikos("Eye", "Hair", data = HairEyeColor, xaxs=TRUE, yaxs=TRUE)

## Called from: eikos_data(y, x, data, marginalize)
